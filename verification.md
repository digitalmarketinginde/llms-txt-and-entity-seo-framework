# Verification

This section explains how to review AI-search performance after the framework is implemented.

AI search changes business development in several ways:
- it affects how a brand is discovered,
- it influences whether the brand is recommended or ignored,
- it shapes how the brand is described,
- it can move buyers closer to a decision before they ever visit the site,
- it can increase or reduce trust in the offer,
- it can influence branded search, direct traffic, assisted conversions, and revenue.

For B2B businesses, this matters because AI search is now part of the research layer of the buyer journey. Buyers use AI tools to compare vendors, ask implementation questions, validate expertise, and narrow down choices before contacting sales.

## What to measure

Do not measure AI visibility only by traffic.

Track a mix of visibility, quality, and business impact signals:
- brand mention frequency,
- citation frequency,
- share of answer / share of voice,
- positioning in the answer,
- sentiment of the mention,
- source quality,
- referral traffic from AI surfaces,
- branded search lift,
- direct traffic lift,
- leads, demo requests, or consultation requests influenced by AI discovery,
- pipeline contribution where attribution is possible.

## How to think about impact

AI search can support business growth in four ways:

### 1. Brand impact
AI can help a brand become more visible, more specific, and more memorable in a category.
If the brand is described accurately and consistently, it becomes easier for buyers to trust it.

### 2. Offer impact
AI can influence how the offer is framed.
If AI understands the offer, it can recommend the right page, the right service, or the right founder at the right moment.

### 3. Website impact
AI can increase the value of core pages when the site is structured clearly.
Well-built pages are more likely to be cited, summarized, or recommended.
This can improve the performance of the homepage, service pages, author pages, and topic hubs.

### 4. Revenue impact
AI visibility can affect lead quality, consultation requests, sales conversations, and pipeline velocity.
A brand that appears in the right answers may receive fewer but better-qualified visitors.

## Query set design

Create test queries in three or four buckets:
- brand queries,
- expert / founder queries,
- category queries,
- comparison queries,
- problem-solution queries,
- commercial-intent queries.

For a B2B business, examples may include:
- who is [brand],
- who is [expert],
- best B2B marketing strategy for SaaS,
- [brand] vs [competitor],
- how to improve pipeline with multichannel marketing,
- how to choose a B2B growth consultant,
- what content helps B2B buyers evaluate a vendor.

Keep the list stable so results can be compared over time.

## Where to check

Review the same query set across the tools your audience actually uses.

Common baseline surfaces:
- ChatGPT,
- Perplexity,
- Gemini,
- Google AI Overviews,
- Claude if relevant to the audience.

If the business has a strong B2B or research-heavy audience, add the platforms where buyers already ask questions and compare options.

## How to analyze answers

For each query, record:
- whether the brand appears,
- whether the expert appears,
- which URL is cited,
- which competitor appears,
- where the brand appears in the answer,
- whether the framing is accurate,
- whether the answer supports the intended positioning,
- whether the answer pushes the user toward the right page.

Use a simple rubric:
- **positive** — correct and strategically useful,
- **neutral** — present but weak or generic,
- **mixed** — partially correct but incomplete or confusing,
- **negative** — wrong, misleading, or competitive loss.

## How to score performance

A practical weekly or monthly scorecard can include:
- visibility rate: how often the brand appears at all,
- citation rate: how often the brand is cited with a source,
- answer share: how often the brand is the primary recommendation,
- sentiment score: how favorable the framing is,
- business relevance score: how close the answer is to the target ICP and offer,
- traffic lift: changes in AI referral, branded search, or direct traffic,
- revenue lift: changes in leads, consultations, demos, or pipeline.

## Where to look for business effect

Check these places together, not separately:
- AI answer tools,
- Google Search Console,
- GA4,
- branded search trends,
- direct traffic trends,
- referral traffic from AI surfaces if visible,
- lead forms,
- CRM or sales pipeline,
- consultation bookings,
- assisted conversion paths.

The goal is to connect AI visibility with commercial outcomes.

## Review cycle

Run a full review:
- before implementation,
- after publishing llms files,
- after major content updates,
- after new hub pages are launched,
- after founder or service pages are improved,
- on a monthly or quarterly schedule depending on content velocity.

## Action framework

After each review, decide what to do next:
- strengthen missing entity signals,
- rewrite weak pages,
- add new hub pages,
- improve comparison or FAQ content,
- refresh outdated content,
- add stronger proof points,
- improve internal linking,
- align content with the ICP and the revenue goal.

## Strategic rule

Do not treat AI-search review as a vanity exercise.

Use it to answer one question:
**Is the business becoming easier for AI systems to understand, recommend, and connect to revenue?**

If the answer is no, the next step is not more reporting.
The next step is better business strategy, better positioning, better ICP focus, and better content architecture.
